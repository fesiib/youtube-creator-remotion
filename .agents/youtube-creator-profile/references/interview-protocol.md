# Interview Protocol

This document covers **how to interview** a creator to populate or expand `creator-profile.md`. It applies to both initial interviews (no profile yet) and explicit updates (revisiting specific sections).

## Two-pass structure

Don't try to fill all 14 sections in one sitting. The interview has two passes.

### Core pass (always)

The core pass exists so the agent can be useful immediately, even if the creator stops there. It covers four sections:

1. **Channel identity** — so the agent knows what universe it's working in.
2. **Voice & writing** — so the agent doesn't write off-brand copy.
3. **Taboos** — so the agent avoids known landmines.
4. **Reference creators** — so the agent has anchors to reason from.

These four answer "what is this, what does it sound like, what should I never do, and who do you measure yourself against." That's enough to start collaborating without obvious misfires.

### Deep dives (optional, section by section)

After the core pass, ask: "Want to keep going section by section, or stop here and let the rest fill in as we work?" Both are valid. The skill's gap-filling Q&A (flow 2) and passive-observation (flow 3) flows handle the rest organically as you collaborate, so there's no urgency to interview every section now.

If the creator wants to continue, walk one section at a time. Each section gets its own short conversation — don't batch.

## Question banks per section

These are starting prompts, not a script. Adapt them to what the creator already volunteered, and don't ask questions the creator has already answered.

### Channel identity

- "If a stranger asked what your channel is in one sentence, what would you say?"
- "Who's the imagined viewer? What are they doing while they watch?"
- "What other channels would your audience also watch?"
- "What does a successful video on your channel do for the viewer that a generic video on the same topic wouldn't?"

### Voice & writing

- "Read me a line from a recent video that felt very *you*."
- "Read me a line from your own work that, looking back, doesn't feel like you."
- "Are there words or phrases you've banned yourself from using?"
- "If a stranger ghostwrote your script, what would tip you off that it wasn't yours?"

### Taboos

- "What's a thing other creators in your space do that you'd never do?"
- "What's a note you've given an editor that you've had to give more than once?"
- "What kind of content/style would feel like a sellout move for your channel?"

### Reference creators

- "Who do you think of as kindred spirits — channels you'd be flattered to be compared to?"
- "Who works in your space but is doing something different from you in a way that matters?"
- "Is there anyone you actively *don't* want to be compared to, even though you're adjacent?"

### Format & rhythm

- "What's your typical length, and how strict is that?"
- "Walk me through the structural template of a typical video — cold open? chapters? outro?"
- "Are there structural moves you almost never do?"

### Hook strategy

- "What's the job of the first 15 seconds for you?"
- "Show me a hook of yours that worked. What made it work?"
- "What kind of hook would you find embarrassing to use, even if it performed well?"

### Cuts & pacing

- "When a moment is funny or emotional, do you cut away fast or let it sit?"
- "Are you a 'every breath cut out' editor or do you leave space?"
- "Show me a 30-second stretch that has the pacing you want as a default."

### B-roll philosophy

- "Is your B-roll mostly literal (showing what's being said) or metaphorical (illustrating the feeling)?"
- "How often does B-roll appear — constant or sparingly?"
- "Stock, original, archival — what's the mix? Anything off-limits?"

### Visual treatment

- "How would you describe the color of your videos to someone who hasn't seen them?"
- "On-screen text — when, why, and in what style?"
- "Motion graphics — love them, hate them, depends?"

### Music & sound design

- "What music genres show up most? Any genre that would feel completely wrong?"
- "Does music ever play under voiceover with lyrics, or never?"
- "Sound effects — are you restrained or playful?"

### Captions & on-screen text

- "Captions — always on, auto-only, or stylized?"
- "Do you emphasize specific words on screen? How?"

### Thumbnails & titles

- "Walk me through your thumbnail formula. Face? Text? Palette?"
- "What's your title style — questions, statements, numbers, curiosity gaps?"
- "Words you reach for in titles? Words you avoid?"

### Recurring bits & motifs

- "Are there bits, openings, or transitions the audience would notice if they were missing?"
- "What's an in-joke or callback that's part of the channel's DNA?"

## Elicitation techniques for hard-to-articulate taste

Many creators have stronger taste than they can describe in words. When abstract questions stall, switch tactics.

### The pair test

Show two concrete options and ask the creator to react. Their reaction is the data.

> "Here are two cold-open openings for the same topic — A: 'Today we're going to talk about X.' B: 'Three years ago, X almost ended my career. Here's what happened.' Which feels more you?"

This works for hooks, music vibes, thumbnail directions, pacing snippets, almost anything.

### The wince test

Ask the creator to describe a recent piece of their own work they wish they could redo. The reasons they give are usually the cleanest articulations of their taste — they're easier to spot in retrospect.

### The off-brand test

Describe an extreme version of a competitor and ask what specifically about it would be wrong for the creator's channel. The answer often surfaces taboos the creator hadn't named.

### The "what would your editor mess up" test

> "If a brand-new editor joined tomorrow and you gave them a clip to cut, what's the first mistake they'd probably make?"

This pulls out implicit rules the creator has never written down.

### Concrete-then-abstract, not the reverse

If the creator says "I just like what I like," don't push for principles. Ask them to point at three videos of theirs they're proud of and three they're not. Then *the agent* writes up the pattern and reads it back: "It looks like the proud ones share X, Y, Z and the not-proud ones share A, B. Sound right?" Their reaction either confirms the synthesis or corrects it. Either way you've made progress.

## Writing up answers

The profile is for the agent's use, not the creator's daily reading. Optimize for clarity over politeness:

- Concrete and specific over generic. "Music is usually lo-fi instrumental hip hop, ~80–95 BPM, no lyrics under voiceover" is far more useful than "chill music."
- Keep it short per section — 3–8 bullets is plenty. The profile gets unwieldy fast if every section is a paragraph.
- Quote the creator verbatim when their phrasing is sharp. Their words tend to be more diagnostic than paraphrases.

## When to stop

Stop when the creator says they're done, or when the next question would be straining for an answer. A profile that's 60% filled and accurate is better than one that's 100% filled and half-fabricated. Empty sections are fine — they get populated over time through gap-filling Q&A when the agent needs an answer to proceed (flow 2), and through passive observation that crosses threshold (flow 3). The profile flags untouched sections under "Open questions" so the agent doesn't fill them in by guessing.

## After the interview

1. Write up the profile following the structure in `profile-template.md`.
2. Save it to the location the creator specified.
3. Add an initial changelog entry: `YYYY-MM-DD — Initial population. (interview, sections N–M)`.
4. Confirm to the creator: "Profile saved to [location]. I'll load it at the start of future sessions and propose updates as we go. Say 'update profile' anytime you want to revisit a section."
